Abstract
In the K-12 school system, there is an increasing need to support student mental health, and teacher well-being. Despite being tasked with playing a larger role in social-emotional well-being, little research has examined how confident teachers are in their ability to support student mental health, or the factors that are associated with these abilities. To address this gap, we examined how teacher mental health self-efficacy varied by grades taught, if mental health self-efficacy differed based on years of teaching experience, and if mental health self-efficacy was associated with teacher burnout. To do this, we analyzed teacher self-report data for 92 Oregon teachers. We found no relation between mental health self efficacy and school level, years of experience, or burnout. Implications of these null findings are discussed.

Writing

Abstract

Introduction

Concerningly, both teachers and students are facing mental health challenges (Agyapong et al. 2022; Disease Control, Prevention, et al. 2023). In attempts to promote student well being, social emotional learning has gained traction (Schwartz et al. 2022). While teachers are being tasked with taking on increased responsibility to promote student mental health, little research has examined how equipped teachers feel to do this. Existing research suggests that teacher training in social-emotional learning and mental health is limited (Schiepe-Tiska, Dzhaparkulova, and Ziernwald 2021).

With proper preparation and support, teachers have the potential to be impactful in preventative mental health support for students. For example, teachers already participate in screening for academic and behavioral challenges, especially with the expansion of multi-tiered systems of support (MTSS), and response to intervention frameworks. Thus, expanding these teacher-led screening procedures to include social-emotional well being could be beneficial. Moreover, schools serve as a centralized resource for connecting and referring families to other community resources. This study seeks to address gaps in the current research on teachers’ ability to support study social-emotional well being by assessing teachers’ self-efficacy in supporting students with mental health concerns.

Research Questions

The current study aims to develop an understanding of teachers’ perceptions of their mental health self-efficacy (MHSE) and burnout. Specifically, we asked the following questions:

  1. Do teacher reports of mental health efficacy differ by school type (i.e., elementary, middle, high school)? 

  2. Is there a relationship between teacher years of experience and teacher reports of mental health efficacy?

  3. Is there a relationship between teacher mental health efficacy and burnout?

Methods

Participants and Recruitment

Oregon K-12 teachers were recruited during Spring 2024 to complete an anonymous, online survey. The goal of this survey was to understand how Oregon teachers viewed their use of emotion regulation, well-being, and mental health self-efficacy in the classroom. Principals across Oregon were contacted and asked if they would be willing to share the survey with their teaching staff. The final sample consisted of 92 teachers. Teacher demographic data can be found in Table 1: Demographic Data.

Measures

School Mental Health Self-Efficacy Teacher Scale (SMH-SETS)

The School Mental Health Self-Efficacy Teacher Scale (SMH-SETS) is a 15-item measure that assesses teachers’ confidence in their ability to recognize, support, and refer students when they experience mental health concerns in the classroom (Brann et al. 2021). Participants reported scores from strongly disagree (1) to strongly agree (6). Previous studies demonstrated that the SMH-SETS has acceptable reliability (Cronbach’s alpha = 0.91) and internal consistency ( = 0.93) (Brann et al. 2021).

Professional Quality of Life (ProQOL) Scale

The Professional Quality of Life (ProQOL) Scale measures positive and negative effects of working in a helping profession (Stamm and Figley 2009). This is a 30-item Likert-type scale, and participants rate questions from never (1) to very often (5). Items 1, 4, 15, 16, and 29 were reverse scored (Stamm and Figley 2009). For this study, the burnout subscale was calculated with items 1, 4, 8, 10, 15, 17, 19, 21, 26, and 29. Per the scoring manual, the average score on the burnout scale is 50 (SD= 10, alpha scale reliability =0.75).

Data Analysis

Research Question 1: Do teacher reports of mental health self-efficacy differ by school type (i.e., elementary, middle, high school)?

We created a violin plot (see Plot 1) to visualize any differences of mental health self-efficacy by school type. Our null hypothesis was that in the population of Oregon K-12 teachers, there is no difference in reports of mental health self-efficacy between elementary, middle, and high school teachers. We tested our null hypothesis via an Analysis of Variance (ANOVA). We also were curious to understand the magnitude by which teachers in different school types differed from each other in their mental health self-efficacy. We conducted an Ordinary Least Squares (OLS) estimation for each school type, with elementary schools as the reference, to interpret the results.

Research Question 2: Is there a relationship between teacher years of experience and teacher reports of mental health self-efficacy?

We created a box plot (see Plot 2) to visualize any differences of teacher MHSE by years of teaching experience. We were curious to understand the magnitude by which teachers’ MHSE differed by their years of teaching experience. We conducted an OLS estimation for teachers by years of teaching experience, with 11+ years of experience as the reference, to interpret the results.

Research Question 3: Is there a relationship between teacher mental health efficacy and burnout?

We created a scatter plot (see Plot 3) to visualize any relationship between teacher MHSE and reported burnout. We were curious to understand whether there was a significant relationship between teacher MHSE and burnout, so we conducted an OLS estimation for teachers’ MHSE and burnout. We also included the following covariates into our model: school level, years of experience, current class (general or special education).

Results

Research Question 1: Do teacher reports of mental health self-efficacy differ by school level (i.e., elementary, middle, high school)?

The violin plot shown in Plot 1 demonstrates no significant differences in average or spread of teacher mental health self efficacy by school level. Further, LINK-model 1 shows that there are no significant associations between teaching at middle school, high school, or multiple levels, and rating of mental health efficacy (p = 0.22, 0.28, and 0.27, respectively). The mean mental health efficacy of elementary teachers is 4.96, for middle school teachers is 4.9, for high school teachers is 5.07, and for teachers who teach multiple grades is 4.98

Research Question 2: Is there a relationship between teacher years of experience and teacher reports of mental health self-efficacy?

The box plot shows in Plot 2 demonstrates no significant differences in average or spread of teacher mental health self efficacy by years of experience. There appears to be a trend of lower self efficacy in teachers who have 6 - 10 years of experience, but Model 2 shows no significant associations by years of experience (p > 0.05 for all predictors)

Research Question 3: Is there a relationship between teacher mental health self-efficacy and burnout?

Upon visual analysis, Plot 3 demonstrates no significant differences in teacher burnout by mental health efficacy. To further explore this potential association, Model 3 included covariates of school level, years of experience, and whether the teacher teaches general or special education. After accounting for these variables, there were still no significant associations between mental health self efficacy and teacher burnout.

Discussion

As student mental health concerns are on the rise, we aimed to explore teachers’ perceptions of their mental health self-efficacy. First, we aimed to understand whether teacher reports of mental health self-efficacy differ by school level. Second, we aimed to understand whether there is any relationship between teachers’ years of experience and their reports of mental health self-efficacy. Third, we aimed to explore any relationship between teacher mental health self-efficacy and burnout. We will highlight general significance of the findings, limitations, and implications for practice and future research.

We found that there is no statistically significant association between the years of experience or school level and teachers’ reports of mental health self-efficacy. Additionally, we did not find a statistically significant relationship between mental health self-efficacy and burnout, even when including covariates (i.e., school level, years of experience, current classroom). Despite the null findings, there are some limitations to this study that could have impacted the results. The sample only consists of 92 teachers, which is a relatively small sample size. Future research should recruit more teachers to better understand any potential relationships. Additionally, the sample is relatively homogeneous (i.e., primarily White, female teachers), and future research should intentionally recruit teachers representing a wider range of identities within the field. Additionally, although not significant at the 0.05 level, working as a special education teacher was approaching significance as a predictor in the relationship between mental health self-efficacy and burnout. Future work should intentionally focus on special education teachers and further explore mechanisms related to burnout. Finally, this data was self-report and cross-sectional, meaning the data represents teachers’ self-perceived mental health self-efficacy. As this is a newer construct, multiple modes of data collection (e.g., multiple raters, observations, in-moment reports, perceptions over time) should be collected in future studies to further understand teachers’ mental health self-efficacy. Overall, to best support teachers ability to support the mental health concerns of students while also prioritizing the well-being of teachers, the field will benefit from continued work in this area.
